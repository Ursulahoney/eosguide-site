What Was DoorDash Accused Of?

The City of Chicago filed a lawsuit in 2021 alleging DoorDash violated the Chicago Municipal Code through several deceptive practices:

1. Listing Restaurants Without Permission

DoorDash added hundreds of Chicago restaurants to its platform without any contractual agreement or consent from the restaurant owners. This meant businesses appeared on the app without their knowledge, potentially damaging their reputation if orders were mishandled.

2. The Misleading "Chicago Fee"

After Chicago capped delivery fees at 15%, DoorDash added a $1.50 "Chicago Fee" to customer orders. The city alleged this fee was misleading because it suggested the charge was government-mandated when it was actually a DoorDash surcharge.

3. Hidden Menu Price Markups

Customers weren't told that menu prices on DoorDash were often higher than ordering directly from the restaurant. This lack of transparency meant diners paid more without realizing it.

4. Tip Misrepresentation

DoorDash used customer tips to subsidize driver wages rather than adding tips on top of base pay. Customers believed their entire tip went to drivers, but DoorDash actually reduced its own payment to drivers by the tip amount—sometimes paying drivers as little as $1 plus tips.

5. Hidden Fees

Service fees, small order fees, and delivery charges were grouped with taxes in a way that obscured the true cost until checkout.

DoorDash's Response

DoorDash released a statement saying:

"We're pleased to have settled this years-old lawsuit with the City of Chicago. To be clear, this settlement isn't an admission of wrongdoing and the allegations in this lawsuit focus on business practices that no longer exist."

The company noted that:

  • They stopped listing restaurants without permission years ago
  • The tip-subsidy practice ended in 2019
  • The "Chicago Fee" is no longer charged
  • They've improved transparency around pricing

What This Means for Small Restaurants

This settlement represents Chicago's commitment to protecting small businesses from unfair practices. During the pandemic, third-party delivery apps became essential for restaurant survival, but many restaurants felt exploited by high fees (often 30% of order totals) and lack of control.

Mayor Brandon Johnson stated:

"This settlement demonstrates Chicago's commitment to standing up for workers and small businesses while maintaining a fair and honest marketplace. Our hospitality industry is critical to our economy, and it works best when companies play by the rules."

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Do I need a lawyer to file a claim?

A: No. This is a straightforward attestation form that eligible restaurants can complete themselves.

Q: What if I can't find my notice of eligibility?

A: Contact the settlement administrator at the email above.

Q: Can I still file if I'm currently a DoorDash partner?

A: No cash payment, but you may be eligible for commission credits if you were initially listed without consent.

Q: What if I owned multiple restaurants?

A: Each eligible location can submit a separate claim.

Q: Is this related to the Illinois Attorney General's DoorDash settlement?

A: Partially. The AG's $11.25 million settlement focused on driver tips statewide. This Chicago settlement also addresses restaurant listings and consumer fees.
